# Nachzehrer

## Overview
The **nachzehrer** (literally "after-devourer") is a type of vampire or revenant from German folklore, particularly northern Germany. Unlike the popular image of vampires leaving their graves to bite victims, the nachzehrer was believed to remain in the grave, chewing its burial shroud and consuming its own flesh, thereby causing family members to waste away and die.

**Status**: Folkloric belief  
**Category**: European Undead  
**Period**: Medieval to Early Modern  
**Region**: Northern Germany, Bavaria

## Description

### Physical Characteristics
The nachzehrer in the grave was believed to exhibit:
- **Bloated Appearance**: Swollen body (natural decomposition)
- **Open Eyes**: Eyes that appeared open
- **Ruddy Complexion**: Flushed or red appearance
- **Consumed Shroud**: Burial clothes chewed or eaten
- **Eaten Flesh**: Evidence of self-consumption
- **Left Eye Open**: Particularly the left eye remaining open
- **Thumb in Mouth**: Sometimes described with thumb between teeth

### Behavior and Powers
- **Shroud Eating**: Chews burial shroud while in grave
- **Self-Consumption**: Devours own flesh
- **Remote Killing**: Causes death of family members from grave
- **Psychic Drain**: Victims waste away without physical contact
- **Disease Spread**: Associated with epidemic spread
- **Bell Ringing**: Some accounts say church bells ring when nachzehrer eats

## Etymology and Names

### Linguistic Origins
- **German**: Nachzehrer (nach = after, zehrer = consumer/devourer)
- **Also**: Nachzährer, Nachzehren
- **Related Terms**: Gierhals (greedy throat), Blautsauger (blood sucker)
- **Regional Variants**: Different names in various German regions

### Meaning
The name literally means "one who devours afterward" or "after-devourer," referring to the creature's continued consumption after death.

## Folklore and Beliefs

### Origin of Nachzehrer
A person could become a nachzehrer through:
- **Epidemic Death**: Dying during plague or epidemic
- **Suicide**: Taking one's own life
- **Improper Burial**: Burial without proper rites
- **Evil Life**: Living wickedly or immorally
- **First to Die**: First death in epidemic
- **Violent Death**: Particularly murder or accident
- **Born with Caul**: Being born with amniotic sac

### How It Kills
The nachzehrer was believed to kill by:
1. **Shroud Consumption**: Chewing burial shroud
2. **Name Calling**: Calling family members' names
3. **Sympathetic Magic**: As shroud consumed, family wastes away
4. **Psychic Connection**: Draining life force of relatives
5. **Disease Transmission**: Spreading plague from grave

### Identification Signs
Suspected nachzehrer graves showed:
- **Chewing Sounds**: Heard from grave
- **Grave Disturbance**: Earth appearing disturbed
- **Family Deaths**: Sequential deaths in family
- **Exhumation Evidence**: Shroud eaten, corpse position changed
- **Decomposition Signs**: Natural decomposition misinterpreted

## Cultural Context

### Plague and Disease
The nachzehrer belief closely tied to epidemic disease:
- **Black Death**: Flourished during plague periods
- **Family Contagion**: Explained why families died together
- **Pre-Germ Theory**: Supernatural explanation for disease spread
- **Sequential Deaths**: Pattern of nachzehrer killing matched disease progression
- **Community Fear**: Terror during epidemic times

### Decomposition Misunderstanding
Many "signs" of nachzehrer were natural processes:
- **Bloating**: Gas buildup from decomposition
- **Blood at Mouth**: Purge fluid from lungs
- **Shroud Damage**: Rodents and decay
- **Body Position**: Settling and movement from decay
- **Sounds**: Gases escaping body

## Prevention and Protection

### Preventive Measures
To prevent nachzehrer, people would:
- **Mouth Objects**: Place coins, stones, or wood in corpse's mouth
- **Secure Jaw**: Tie jaw shut to prevent chewing
- **Decapitation**: Sever head before burial
- **Staking**: Drive stake through body
- **Proper Burial**: Ensure correct funeral rites
- **Face Down**: Bury face-down so nachzehrer eats earth
- **Religious Rites**: Blessing and proper Christian burial

### Dealing with Active Nachzehrer
If nachzehrer suspected, remedies included:
- **Exhumation**: Dig up and examine corpse
- **Beheading**: Decapitate the corpse
- **Heart Removal**: Extract and burn heart
- **Impalement**: Stake through body or head
- **Reburial**: Bury in consecrated ground with rites
- **Scythe at Neck**: Place scythe to prevent rising

## Related Creatures

### German Vampire Types
- **Nachzehrer**: Shroud-eater in grave
- **Alp**: Night demon that causes nightmares
- **Doppelsauger**: Double-sucker, attacks living
- **Blutsauger**: Blood-sucker, more active vampire
- **Neuntöter**: Kills nine family members

### Slavic Parallels
- **Polish Upiór**: Similar vampire beliefs
- **Czech Upír**: Eastern European vampire
- **Moravian Vampires**: Closely related traditions

### Similarities and Differences
Unlike Slavic vampires:
- **Stays in Grave**: Doesn't physically rise and walk
- **Shroud Focus**: Emphasizes shroud eating
- **Family Targets**: Specifically kills relatives
- **Disease Association**: Strongly linked to plague

## Scientific and Rational Explanations

### Natural Phenomena
Nachzehrer "evidence" explained by:
- **Decomposition**: Natural body changes after death
- **Gases**: Bloating and movement from gas buildup
- **Purge Fluid**: Liquids at mouth mimicking blood
- **Rodents**: Animals damaging shrouds
- **Premature Burial**: Rare cases of live burial
- **Coffin Movement**: Body settling in coffin

### Disease Understanding
The belief reflected:
- **Contagion Patterns**: Disease spreading through families
- **Incubation Periods**: Delayed illness onset
- **Genetic Susceptibility**: Family members sharing vulnerabilities
- **Living Conditions**: Shared environment spreading disease
- **Pre-Scientific Medicine**: Lack of germ theory

### Psychological Factors
- **Grief and Trauma**: Processing loss during epidemics
- **Pattern Seeking**: Finding meaning in tragedy
- **Cultural Framework**: Interpreting reality through folklore
- **Community Cohesion**: Shared beliefs during crisis
